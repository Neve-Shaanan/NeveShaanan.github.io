Hipster

Viktor Ivanovich calls himself a village priest, an amateur astronomer and... a hipster. All these roles underline his self-sufficiency: “Pounding the pavement of the authorities is not my natural habitat. It’s better for me to do it all by myself than to go to the boss ... I actually go, if I need to.”


Viktor Ivanovich's initiative often stays laid on him. The brothers do not judge him, they help, but without a ginger. But Viktor Ivanovich wants to spread the idea of planetariums everywhere in communities. He even taught a special course for young Baptists on how to build planetariums and teach astronomy, but no one followed him.


Civic teachers and principals look on Christianity in the planetarium with skepticism. They brought children, but asked the priest not to talk about God. Viktor Ivanovich says that sometimes he cheats: he doesn't talk about God himself, but turns on the “tape player” — recordings of creationist lectures.


The hipster from Krasnooktyabrsky has his own village version of creationism “for the people”. Matyushin wrote thirty small booklets about biblical characters — Daniel, Saul, Job, Abel and others. He gives these books to visitors, and they, if they want, donate to the planetarium — as much as they can.


I am writing this in a simple way. I have read the Bible fifty times, cover to cover. There are sixty-six separate books, and you need a professional to know about Samuel, for example. I am sometimes told: "We have professionals, theologians." They say, "It's like baby talk." But I'm glad because I want the common man to understand what the Bible says.


The baby talk-style understandable to ordinary people is what distinguishes the hipster who built the Christian planetarium.

Historically, Protestants, including Baptists, have considered religion a personal matter. A person independently built a relationship with God, without the participation of the church and the priest. Viktor Ivanovich says that there is no general version of Christianity, it is suitable for everyone, depending on where someone was born and lives.


The common people need a simple explanation. But let people down that everyone is like Christ...Not sure. The general is correct, but if someone says: “I will be like Christ,” then this is already a violation, because there is no second Christ. In this sense, each and to the extent it can be, where he lives, what kind of nation, they should join, at least approximately, to the idea of "self-control." It’s impossible to build an ideal teaching, it must speak individually for everyone. And individually to the nation.


The village priest explains that the Bible potentially has everything and is written for everyone. The text consists of many layers, therefore it can be correctly understood in any situation, even when the dinosaurs and black holes are involved. You just need to believe in it. The danger of misunderstanding, of course, remains, but Viktor Ivanovich believes that mistakes have their own benefits. They can point you in the right direction.


Sometimes, at first sight the ceremony goes decently well. And then it turns out: something is not right. After all, it's not clear for everyone. The man came up with: God revealed to me like this. And you feel something is not right. Start criticizing him? Why? He gave you a boost. Think, how was that! He's good, that said this. So, the development comes from this… Some people are outraged, who do not know: “Why is he talking? Why did you let him in?" And you do not criticize, but tell me the right way.


Here, in the planetarium, hipster Viktor Ivanovich is not afraid to make a mistake. If he explains something wrong about stars and black holes, this may push someone else to the correct version.

Kids are flying


Late-late night, after the telescope watching of the Moon, Jupiter and Saturn, we enter the planetarium. The lights go out, and Viktor Ivanovich begins to show the stellar mystery via home-made equipment. The device consists of a ball with holes — stars and constellations. The ball is illuminated by a lamp, and the image is projected onto the dome. Viktor Ivanovich rotates the ball, and the viewer finds himself in one or another part of the Earth — depending on what kind of sky is above him — the Northern Hemisphere, the Southern Hemisphere, the North Pole, etc.


Usually kids are flying. Now, if you believe that the stars are standing still, and the floor is spinning around, then you get the impression that the stars are standing, and the floor is spinning. One two three! But it is necessary to set yourself on so that the floor spins and the stars stand. It doesn't always work. We click the space speed. According to the Bible it is written: the sky will roll up into a scroll and fly away. One two three!


The village priest spins the ball — and the stars circle under the dome in an incredible dance. Head spinning, and in the dark it seems that you are flying. “That's all,” says Viktor Ivanovich, “and we are returning ...” I tell him that for the first time I felt like I was flying. Viktor Ivanovich explains that children usually fly, but it is difficult for adults because they do not believe that they can fly.


About "fly" I have not heard anywhere. This is what I came up with. But the mood is necessary. Kindergarten absolutely flies. Here comes the kindergarten — thirty people. I'm setting them up so that the floor is on wheels. I turn it on — they literally squeak, because they feel that they are flying. Right mindset. Faith. This is the faith of a child. They trusted me and flew. An adult will think: “How will he do it?” And he starts to fly, but then he digresses for a minute, and that's it — all gone.


The terrible apocalyptic phrase “and the heavens rolled up like a scroll” from the Book of the Prophet Isaiah under the dome of a homemade Planetarium becomes a childish game of faith and knowledge, faith in a miracle and knowledge of the starry sky. It seems to work for more than just kids.
